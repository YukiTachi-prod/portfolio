The mining industry is a rather complex field. It has the power to be a strong driving force of an economy while posing the same magnitude of risks for the environment and ecosystems involved. It is a double-edged sword, really. In the Philippines, one of the world's most mineral-rich countries, this tension is more pronounced.

I would associate the benefits of mining to lean more on the economic side of things, while most of the risks and negative effects are often dumped into the environment and those that live in it.

The Philippines is the world's second-largest producer of nickel and sits on vast reserves of copper and gold. This allows the mining industry to significantly contribute to the national economy, with it contributing approximately ₱264.8 billion in 2025 alone. This feat alone cements its relevance to the Philippines' economy, not to mention it's contribution to fields such as foreign exchange, local developments (companies must allocate 1.5% of operating costs to a Social Development and Management Program as per the Philippines Mining Act of 1995), and employment for the masses, but despite all this, it poses a threat equal—if not more detrimental to the ecosystem.


Tailings or waste leaks form the said mines pose the most threat. By 2026, mining-related incidents are estimated to contribute to 30% of water pollution in key Philippines river basins. This is not only effecting the environment itself but also the people and animals that are living in it. An example of this tragic event is the 1996 Marcopper Disaster in Marinduque, where tailings leak spilled millions of tons of waste into the Boac River, effectively declaring it biologically dead and causing decades of health issues for locals.
Not to mention the biodiversity loss that happens when the topsoil is completely removed along with vegetation.

I'm not saying that all mining activities should be stopped, but these types of delicate operations need to have the appropriate care that it demands. Philippines is a treasure trove of minerals and natural resources, I get that, and the people are free to use these resources. But just as freely as they take from the environment, they should give the corresponding love and care that the environment wants from them. The people should be more careful when it comes to mining, to make sure that there is no spillage of waste, as well as a complete restoration of the site. This is to ensure that the ecosystems live on, even after the desecration of their land and habitat.
